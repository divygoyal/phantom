## 1. What this playbook produces

Punchy 35–75 second reels that feel:
- sharp, researched, founder/tech-native
- high-retention, slightly provocative but not clickbait
- easy enough for a normal person to follow
- useful enough that founders/creators/builders save and share

Strong on:
- AI tool launches, SaaS ideas, product demos, creator tools
- Tech / business drama, "this looks like a toy, this is actually a business" framings
- Workflow automations, open-source → SaaS reframes
- India-tech-context layered onto global news

---

## 2. The 6-step argument arc

```
Big claim
→ simple context
→ how it works
→ why it matters
→ caveat / twist
→ bigger takeaway
```

The reel should never feel like a generic AI script. It should feel like someone found a hidden business or technology shift and is explaining it fast to a friend who's smart but hasn't seen it yet.

### Tone

Use:
- confident but not cringe
- conversational but smart
- founder brain
- simple words
- short sentences
- one strong opinion
- one caveat
- one memorable final line

Avoid:
- "In today's fast-paced world"
- "This revolutionary tool"
- "Game-changing" unless used sparingly
- overhyped marketing language
- fake urgency
- fake statistics
- too many emojis
- "like and follow" without explicit channel-strategy reason

## 3. Opening hook formulas

Pick one based on the topic shape.

### A. Company-just-did-something

```text
[Company] just launched [thing], and it might quietly change [workflow/category].
```

*Example*: `OpenAI just made image generation useful for businesses, not just memes.`

### B. Tool-is-not-what-people-think

```text
Everyone thinks [tool] is for [obvious use], but the real opportunity is [hidden use].
```

*Example*: `Everyone thinks AI music generators are for songs, but the real opportunity is creator music packs.`

### C. Product-death / replacement

```text
[Old workflow/tool] is cooked.
[New workflow/tool] just made it optional.
```

*Example*: `Basic product demo videos are cooked. AI can now turn one screenshot into a full launch asset pack.`

### D. Hidden-business-opportunity

```text
This looks like a tiny AI feature, but it could become a real SaaS.
```

*Example*: `This looks like a simple before-after video tool, but it could become a real creator SaaS.`

### E. Shock + explain

```text
[Surprising statement].
And no, the interesting part is not [obvious thing].
```

*Example*: `A single product image can now become five ad videos. And no, the interesting part is not the video model.`

### F. Real-pain hook

```text
If you've ever tried to [painful workflow], you already know how broken this is.
```

*Example*: `If you've ever tried to make a clean SaaS demo video, you already know how annoying this is.`

### G. Hedge-narrowing hook (validated veo4vs)

```text
[Company] just dropped [tool], and it might have just killed [specific narrow category].
```

*Example*: `Google just dropped Veo 4, and it might have just killed boring educational videos.`

Why it works:
- "might have just killed" is the **two-word hedge** — confident but not absolutist.
- "boring educational videos" narrows the claim to a specific category (not "all teachers"). This sets up a **Pattern B topic-meta rebuttal** at the close: "teachers didn't become optional. Bad educational content did."
- Saves the absolutist version for the close-callback.

## 4. Script architecture template

```text
[HOOK]

At first, this looks like [surface-level interpretation].

But the real shift is [deeper insight].

Here's how it works:
[step 1]
[step 2]
[step 3]

That means [specific user/person] can now [valuable outcome]
without [old painful process].

But here's the caveat:
[limitation / truth / risk].

So the opportunity is not [surface-level idea].
It's [bigger category / workflow / business].
```

### Ideal length

- 35–45s → 110–140 words
- 45–60s → 150–190 words
- 60–75s → 200–240 words

Use 160–190 words by default.

## 5. The credible-caveat rule (MANDATORY)

**Every SaaS-angle script must include one credibility line that acknowledges what the new tool/workflow CAN'T do.** Without it, the reel reads as an ad, not editorial.

Good caveat shapes (validated):

```text
But here's the caveat: the output is only useful if the workflow around it is good.

Having said that, this is not replacing designers. It is replacing the first ugly draft.

Now obviously, this is not fully automatic yet. The taste layer still matters.

But the model alone is not the product. The product is the system built around it.
```

### The veo4vs-validated form

```text
But here's the twist.
Neither of these tools is actually teaching.
They're performing.
The hard part is still the lesson: [4-item enumeration].
```

Why this is the strongest version of the caveat:
- **"But here's the twist."** — setup-payoff structure. Buys the viewer's attention BEFORE the reveal lands.
- **"They're performing."** — single-word verb reveal beats long metaphor ("an actor with a chalkboard" overwrites the surprise).
- **The 4-item enumeration** of what teachers actually do — concrete, viewer-relatable, names the human work that AI can't replace.

Critique-rubrics.md auto-fail #31 (added 2026-05-12): SaaS-angle scripts without a credible caveat are rejected on Phase 3.

## 6. Recurring transition phrases

Use 2–3 per script. **Don't stack them.** Don't open every beat with one.

- "But here's the thing…"
- "And this is where it gets interesting…"
- "At first, this sounds like…"
- "But the real shift is…"
- "Which means…"
- "Now obviously…"
- "Having said that…"
- "The broader point is…"
- "And that's the part people are missing."
- "So the real opportunity is…"
- **"But here's the twist."** (validated veo4vs — setup-payoff)

## 7. Content buckets

### Bucket 1: AI-tool-breakdown

```text
[Tool] just launched [feature].
Most people will use it for [obvious thing].
But the real use case is [practical workflow].
Here's how it changes [creator/founder/team workflow].
Caveat.
Bigger takeaway.
```

### Bucket 2: SaaS-opportunity-from-AI-feature

```text
This looks like a feature.
But packaged correctly, it could become a SaaS.
The user does not want [raw capability].
They want [finished outcome].
Build the workflow around that.
```

### Bucket 3: Founder-workflow-automation

```text
I replaced [manual workflow] with [AI workflow].
Input: [what you give]
Process: [what AI does]
Output: [what you get]
Impact: [time/cost saved]
Caveat: [where humans still matter]
```

### Bucket 4: Tech / business conflict (validated veo4vs)

```text
[Company A] just attacked/undercut/competed with [Company B].
Here's what happened. (or: here are the two outputs side by side)
Here's why it matters financially/strategically.
Here's what people are missing. (the caveat/twist)
Final question / topic-meta rebuttal.
```

The veo4vs reel followed this bucket exactly. Adapt for any "Veo 4 vs Seedance" / "Anthropic vs OpenAI" / "Adobe vs Figma" / category-comparison topic.

### Bucket 5: Consumer-product-made-simple

```text
This product solves [annoying everyday problem].
Here's how it works.
Here's why it is clever.
Here's what it costs.
Here's who it is actually for.
```

## 8. Visual direction defaults

- Start with the most shocking screenshot / demo / result.
- Company / tool logo visible in first 1s.
- Screen recordings for "how it works."
- Before/after for transformations.
- Numbered overlays for features.
- A "BUT" frame before the caveat.
- End with a clean takeaway line on screen.

Default visual sequence:

```text
0–2s    : shocking result / product screenshot / claim text
2–8s    : context visuals
8–25s   : demo / steps
25–40s  : implication / business angle
40–55s  : caveat / twist
55–65s  : final takeaway
```

## 10. Ending-line formulas

Pick one. Match the chosen one to the hook claim (Pattern A = prediction; Pattern B = topic-meta rebuttal callback; Pattern C = action-prompt).

```text
That's the part people are missing.

The model is not the product. The workflow is.

The bottleneck is no longer creation. It's taste and distribution.

This looks like a feature today, but packaged correctly, it becomes a SaaS.

So the question is not whether this works. The question is who turns it into a repeatable product first.

And that's where the real opportunity is.
```

### Pattern B callback (validated veo4vs — strongest)

```text
So no, [hook claim] didn't [verb].
[Specific narrowing] did.
```

*Example*: `So no, teachers didn't become optional. Bad educational content did.`

Why this is the strongest close pattern:
- Inverts the hook claim (which was deliberately overstated at the open).
- Lands the *actual* claim, narrower and more defensible.
- Viewer's gut reaction is "oh shit, that's the real point" — buys the share-impulse.

Pair with the **hedge-narrowing hook (G)** at the open. Hook overclaims slightly → close corrects with the real claim. The arc is the argument.

## 11. Quality checklist (before Phase 3 critique)

- [ ] Does the first line create curiosity?
- [ ] Is the topic understandable in 5 seconds?
- [ ] Is there a "but here's the thing" twist?
- [ ] Is there one clear business or workflow insight?
- [ ] Is there one credibility caveat so it doesn't sound like an ad?
- [ ] Can the viewer explain the idea to a friend after watching?
- [ ] Is the ending stronger than "follow for more"?
- [ ] Does it feel original, not copied?

---

## 12. Do NOT do this

- full copied transcripts from any creator
- exact creator-specific catchphrases
- "write exactly like [creator]" impersonation
- fake facts / fake company news
- unverifiable stats presented as fact
- exaggerated claims without caveat
- generic influencer lines

When facts are uncertain:

```text
If this is true...

The interesting part is not whether every number is perfect, but the direction this points to.
```
